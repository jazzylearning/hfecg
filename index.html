🏥 Cardiac Rehabilitation Programs

Beyond the pill: integrating a multidisciplinary care team to implement individualised management strategies for patients with advanced heart failure

Authors: KF Wong, TC Wong, YY Fan, SL Wong, KY Cheng, TY Fu, TKC Wong, WH Cheng, J Wong, KK Ho, C Wong, E Wong, MY Lee, KL Wong

Population studied: 103 advanced heart failure patients; mean LVEF 25.8 ± 8.8%; mean age 53.5 ± 10.5 years; 78% male; 28% ischemic cardiomyopathy. Setting: Grantham Hospital, Hong Kong.

Main results and insights:
  • Program combined intensive medication titration, supervised centre-based exercise training, health education, dietary counseling, and ADL training.
  • 6-minute walk distance increased significantly by +8.5% (p<0.001).
  • LVEF improved by +43.3% (25.8 → 37.0%); NT-proBNP fell −38.8% (p<0.001).
  • KCCQ quality-of-life score improved +12.1% (p<0.001); frailty score reduced −38.8% (p<0.01).
  • HF-related hospitalizations decreased −87.9% versus the year before enrolment (p<0.001).

Safety and feasibility of tele-cardiac rehabilitation (tele-CR) using a remote biological signal monitoring system in Oku-Noto after the 2024 Noto Peninsula earthquake

Authors: M Wakasa, M Miharu, U Eiichi, S Jun, T Taketsugu

Population studied: 21 patients with chronic heart failure living in the disaster-affected Noto Peninsula, Japan, after the 2024 earthquake.

Main results and insights:
  • 3-month tele-CR program with a wearable ECG device (hitoe®) and remote biological signal monitoring.
  • Borg scale fell from 14.8 ± 2.1 → 13.0 ± 1.5 (p=0.0002); NYHA class from 2.2 ± 0.4 → 1.7 ± 0.7 (p=0.0001).
  • 90% of participants completed ≥30 min of moderate-intensity exercise per week — high adherence.
  • No cardiac events, HF exacerbations, or readmissions during the study; stable BP, HR, weight, BNP.
  • Insight: Tele-CR is a feasible and safe alternative when hospital-based rehab is disrupted (e.g., disasters, rural settings).

💪 Exercise Training Interventions

Effects of a 12-week supervised multicomponent training on skeletal muscle mitochondrial function in patients with HFpEF

Authors: A Martinez-Dominguez, M Gutierrez-Garcia, S Oscoz-Ochandorena, M Izquierdo, R Ramirez-Velez

Population studied: 25 patients with HFpEF; mean age 66 ± 9.7 years; exercise group n=16 vs control n=9.

Main results and insights:
  • 12-week supervised multicomponent training significantly improved skeletal muscle mitochondrial respiratory function vs. controls.
  • Complex I-linked respiration: +24.1 pmol O₂·s⁻¹·mg⁻¹ (95% CI 8.4–39.8; Cohen's d = 1.66).
  • Complex II-linked respiration: +10.5 pmol O₂·s⁻¹·mg⁻¹ (d = 1.36).
  • Oxidative phosphorylation respiration: +25.9 pmol O₂·s⁻¹·mg⁻¹ (d = 1.49).
  • Maximal electron transport capacity: +20.9 pmol O₂·s⁻¹·mg⁻¹ (d = 1.13).
  • Insight: Peripheral mitochondrial plasticity is a key mechanism behind exercise benefits in HFpEF, supporting supervised training as a vital non-pharmacological therapy.

🫁 Exercise Capacity & CPET

Association of BMI with cardiopulmonary efficiency and ventricular remodeling in advanced heart failure

Authors: N Andjelkovic, D Kosevic, J Stefanovic Neskovic, J Miladinovic, B Milovanovic, U Radak, N Jovicic, M Bojic, S Borovic

Population studied: 39 consecutive patients with advanced HF referred to a tertiary HF center; predominantly lean (mean BMI 19.0 ± 5.6 kg/m²).

Main results and insights:
  • BMI showed a significant positive correlation with the oxygen uptake efficiency slope (OUES: r = 0.41, p = 0.010).
  • BMI inversely correlated with RV EDV (r = −0.38, p = 0.018) and LV EDV (r = −0.34, p = 0.032).
  • In multivariable analysis (age, sex), BMI remained independently associated with OUES (β = +0.06, p = 0.021).
  • Insight: In advanced HF, lower BMI is a marker of more severe remodeling and impaired functional capacity — nutritional status is closely linked to exercise performance.

Delayed referral to advanced heart failure centers is associated with established RV dysfunction and severe functional impairment in HFrEF

Authors: D Kosevic, J Stefanovic Neskovic, J Miladinovic, B Milovanovic, U Radak, N Andjelkovic, S Borovic, M Bojic

Population studied: 39 consecutive HFrEF patients referred from regional hospitals to a tertiary HF center in Serbia.

Main results and insights:
  • RV systolic function markedly impaired at referral: TAPSE 11.1 ± 3.8 mm; FAC 19.0 ± 5.6%.
  • CMR showed RV dilatation (mean RV EDV 213 ± 61 mL).
  • CPET-derived functional capacity was severely reduced: mean OUES 1.52 ± 0.56.
  • Larger RV volumes correlated with lower OUES (r = −0.37, p = 0.022).
  • Insight: Delayed specialist referral results in substantial functional deterioration and RV failure, limiting therapeutic options — earlier referral could preserve exercise capacity.

Exercise capacity and hemodynamic response during exercise depend on the extent of left ventricular remodeling in HFrEF

Authors: K Bratis, G Velissaris, M Pentaris, D Chatzis, N Papaioannou, S Papaioannou, S Mykoniati, Z Morou, I Dena, N Biagkis, S Tomaras, E Panagiotopoulos, I Tsallos, V Stavropoulou, A Ntalianis

Population studied: 25 HFrEF patients at Alexandra University Hospital, Athens. Mean age 67 ± 5 years; NYHA 2.3 ± 0.8; LVEF 30 ± 5%.

Main results and insights:
  • Combined CMR + exercise right-heart catheterization + CPET assessment.
  • LVEDV positively associated with VO₂max, VE/VCO₂ slope, OUES, and mean PAP at rest and peak exercise.
  • LVESV positively associated with OUES (r = 0.566, p = 0.035).
  • Sphericity Index associated with VO₂max, peak PAP, peak PCWP, and peak SvO₂.
  • Relative Wall Thickness inversely related to VO₂max, % predicted VO₂max, OUES, and resting PAP.
  • Insight: LV geometry and size strongly influence exercise capacity and hemodynamic response — remodeling parameters help stratify exercise prognosis.

Oxygen uptake recovery and impaired cardiac reserve capacity: interaction with tau parameter

Authors: G Crisci, V Serrantoni, M Losito, G Federico, M Guazzi

Population studied: 172 subjects — 129 HF patients (55 HFrEF, 74 HFpEF) and 43 healthy controls, referred to a tertiary dyspnea clinic (Jan 2024–Jan 2025).

Main results and insights:
  • Peak VO₂ significantly lower in HF vs controls (HFpEF 16.1 ± 3.2; HFrEF 14.9 ± 4.6; controls 22.8 ± 5.6 mL/min/kg).
  • VO₂ recovery delay parameter τ (tau) elevated in HF: HFpEF 232.2, HFrEF 157.9, controls 100.
  • τ strongly correlated with peak VO₂ (HFpEF r=0.75; HFrEF r=0.60; p<0.001).
  • τ also correlated with VE/VCO₂ slope (HFpEF r=0.53; HFrEF r=0.54; p<0.001).
  • Insight: Recovery VO₂ kinetics (τ) is a simple, noninvasive marker that complements standard CPET parameters for assessing circulatory impairment.

Skeletal muscle fatty acid oxidation capacity independently associates with cardiorespiratory fitness in HFpEF

Authors: A Martinez-Dominguez, M Gutierrez-Garcia, S Oscoz-Ochandorena, M Izquierdo, R Ramirez-Velez

Population studied: 40 adults with HFpEF; mean age 66.6 ± 9.6 years.

Main results and insights:
  • Skeletal muscle fatty acid oxidation (FAO) capacity was significantly associated with VO₂peak (unadjusted: B=0.460; β=0.566; p<0.001; R²=0.320).
  • After adjustment for age and sex, FAO remained an independent predictor (B=0.374; β=0.461; p<0.001; adjusted R²=0.59).
  • Every 1 pmol O₂·s⁻¹·mg⁻¹ higher FAO capacity → +0.37 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ higher VO₂peak.
  • Male sex independently associated with higher VO₂peak (β=0.543; p<0.001); age non-significant trend.
  • Insight: Skeletal muscle mitochondrial oxidative metabolism is a clinically relevant determinant of functional capacity and a potential therapeutic target in HFpEF.

🚶 6-Minute Walk Test & Functional Assessment

Iron deficiency's association with stroke and TIA in patients with heart failure

Authors: P Kielbowicz, A Baszkowski, Y Kosegarten, J Piotrkowska, J Witkowska, M Sawoscian, M Lelonek

Population studied: 259 HF patients (126 F, 133 M) hospitalized 2018–2025; median age 72 [IQR 66–79]; 51% with iron deficiency (ID).

Main results and insights:
  • Patients with ID had significantly shorter 6MWT distance: 259.0 m vs 280.0 m (p=0.0423).
  • Stroke/TIA prevalence more than double in ID group (15.83% vs 6.67%, p=0.0204).
  • ID group also had lower GFR, lower RBC indices, lower uric acid; PPI use higher (45.3% vs 25.0%, p=0.0007).
  • ID + anaemia → higher NT-proBNP (1408 vs 652, p=0.0034) and higher PASP (43 vs 40 mmHg, p=0.0211).
  • Insight: Iron deficiency impairs functional capacity and is linked to a higher cerebrovascular risk — supports proactive ID screening and treatment.

Low transferrin saturation predicts mortality in HFrEF across anemia strata: a registry-based IPTW analysis

Authors: H Nahoui, H Schirmer, K Berge, T Oevrebotten, PL Myhre

Population studied: 12,523 patients (mean age 69 years; 74% men) with HFrEF (EF ≤45%) from the Norwegian Heart Failure Registry, outpatient HF clinics.

Main results and insights:
  • Low TSAT (<20%) associated with significantly shorter 6MWD (β = −27.0 m; 95% CI −32.8 to −21.3; p<0.001).
  • Low TSAT also linked to higher log-NT-proBNP (β=0.33; p<0.001) and worse MLHFQ scores (β=2.70; p<0.001).
  • Mortality risk: HR 1.54 overall; stronger in non-anaemic patients (HR 1.63) than anaemic patients (HR 1.19).
  • An increase in TSAT over time was associated with longer 6MWD (β = +0.476 m per 1% TSAT increase).
  • Insight: Iron deficiency is a strong predictor of poor physical function and mortality regardless of anaemia — restoring iron status improves 6MWD.

Telemonitoring in heart failure improves cardiometabolic risk-factor control: real-world signals of enhanced adherence

Authors: T Aguiar, C Costa, I Cruz, M Silva, S Carvalho, J Ribeiro, JM Bastos, A Briosa

Population studied: 31 consecutive HF patients in Portugal; 77% male; mean age 67; mean LVEF 34%; 81% ischemic. Before-after design (12 months before vs after telemonitoring).

Main results and insights:
  • Significant reduction in NYHA class (mean −0.4; p<0.01) — improved functional status.
  • Annual hospitalizations reduced by 0.7 (p<0.01); total hospital days by 5.8 (p<0.01).
  • LVEF increased by +6.4% (p<0.01).
  • LDL-C reduced by −30.6 mg/dL (p<0.01); HbA1c in diabetics reduced by −0.6 %-points (p<0.05).
  • 60% of baseline smokers achieved cessation at 12 months; 67% of alerts resolved without in-person care.
  • Insight: Telemonitoring drives lifestyle modification and functional improvement, with strongest benefits in patients with high baseline event burden.

📱 Physical Activity & Lifestyle

At-home physical activity and mortality risk in patients with heart failure and obesity: insights from wrist-worn accelerometry in NHANES

Authors: A Sharma, R Ceskoutse, G Perlman, O Kose

Population studied: 300 adults with HF in NHANES with valid wrist-worn accelerometry recordings.

Main results and insights:
  • Activity quantified via Monitor Independent Movement Summary (MIMS) and Signal Vector Magnitude (SVM90, near-maximal activity intensity).
  • Unadjusted activity (volume + intensity) was similar in HF patients with vs without obesity (all p>0.24).
  • After adjustment for age, sex, and wear time, obesity was associated with lower activity intensity (SVM90: β = −2.4 × 10⁻⁴, p=0.022).
  • Obesity did not modify the prognostic link between physical activity and all-cause / CV mortality (p-interaction = 0.91).
  • Insight: Free-living activity volume is preserved across obesity status in HF, but obesity reduces intensity — SVM90 may be a novel monitoring marker.

From prediction to phenotype: machine learning classification of heart failure and its relation to imaging-derived LV structure in the UK Biobank

Authors: M Hoang, G Slabaugh, N Aung

Population studied: 502,129 UK Biobank participants (2,731 prevalent HF cases); separate CMR sub-population free from HF.

Main results and insights:
  • ML models (XGBoost, LightGBM, Elastic Net) classified HF with AUC frequently >0.90; XGBoost specificity 99%, Elastic Net sensitivity 97%.
  • Sedentary behaviour ranked among the top 21 discriminative features for HF classification across all ML models, alongside age, hypertension, and atrial fibrillation.
  • In HF-free participants, higher resting heart rate was linked to lower EF (−1.18% per SD), smaller LV volumes, lower LV mass, and poorer functional indices (LVGFI, LVMCF).
  • Insight: Lifestyle factors like sedentary behavior carry a biologically coherent signal of subclinical LV remodeling — supports physical activity promotion as primary prevention.

ICD/CRT-based strategies and non-invasive wearable devices for HF activity monitoring (Koehler)

Authors: Friedrich Koehler (presenter); refs Noci, Capodici, Nuti, Passino, Emdin, Giannoni

Population studied: 1,823 HF patients in a systematic review; cohorts spanning HFrEF with CRT-D/ICD and HFpEF/non-implant patients.

Main results and insights:
  • Wearables (accelerometers, actigraphy, IMU) track physical activity and exercise tolerance; reduced activity and increased sedentary time precede HF decompensation.
  • Wearable technologies predict HF relapse and impending hospitalizations up to 30 days in advance.
  • Systematic review: 78–87% reduction in HF hospitalizations in observational studies; 89% reduction in one RCT.
  • Multimodal monitoring combines HR/HRV (autonomic imbalance), cardiac mechanics (S3 vibrations), pulmonary congestion (bioimpedance/dielectric sensing), and daily activity.
  • Insight: Converting physiological signals — including activity levels — into rapid clinical actions enables effective device-based decongestion.

🦴 Frailty & Sarcopenia

More than muscle loss: sarcopenia as a marker of disease severity in ambulatory heart failure

Authors: S Gonzalez Sosa, PJ Mendoza Gil, JM Garcia Vallejo, JA Rodriguez Gonzalez, A Conde Martel

Population studied: 150 ambulatory HF patients in a Spanish tertiary HF Unit; mean age 83.0 ± 9.3 years; 62% male.

Main results and insights:
  • Probable sarcopenia (SARC-F + handgrip / sit-to-stand impairment) present in 54.0% (81 patients).
  • Sarcopenia → greater functional dependence, higher frailty, poorer cognitive and nutritional status (all p<0.05).
  • Independent associations with atrial fibrillation (OR 3.07; 95% CI 1.11–8.53; p=0.031) and higher NT-proBNP.
  • NYHA I–II patients much less likely to be sarcopenic (OR 0.36; 95% CI 0.15–0.88; p=0.025).
  • Sarcopenic patients had higher CRP, troponin T, CA-125 and lower albumin, cholesterol, haemoglobin.
  • Trend toward higher 6-month hospitalisation and mortality (non-significant).
  • Insight: Sarcopenia is a marker of overall disease severity — screening should be embedded in routine HF assessment.

Factors associated with non-fatal discontinuation of a heart failure self-monitoring program in a rural super-aging Japanese city

Authors: K Yonezu, S Saito, H Yamasaki, T Zaizen, M Hara, K Takahashi, S Kodama, T Ono, K Masutomo, H Zaizen, S Nobe, O Yoshiga, M Inoue, Y Tatsukawa, N Takahashi

Population studied: 663 HF patients (from 1,508 registered) in a rural Japanese prefecture (aging rate 34.4%); median age 79; 59% male.

Main results and insights:
  • 80% (n=531) continued the self-monitoring program ≥1 year; 20% (n=132) discontinued.
  • Discontinuation group had higher frailty scores (median 4 vs 3, p=0.0028) and more functional dependency (Barthel Index <85: 55% vs 39%, p=0.0022).
  • Discontinuers less likely to engage in hobbies (19% vs 47%, p<0.0001) or self-record HF symptoms (80% vs 88%, p=0.035).
  • Independent predictors of discontinuation: advanced age (OR 1.08/yr), NT-proBNP >3555 pg/mL (OR 2.92), Barthel Index <85 (OR 2.78).
  • Insight: Reduced ADLs and frailty are major barriers to maintaining self-management — vulnerable elderly patients need tailored support, including physical activity engagement.
